Foundation · 01
What Is a Sequence?

Your sales team's call cadence — automated, measured, and connected to the CRM. Emails sent from the rep's own inbox, tasks created automatically, and the whole thing stops the moment the contact replies.

Core definition

A HubSpot Sequence is a personalised, automated outreach cadence — a series of timed emails, call tasks, and LinkedIn tasks that execute automatically after a rep enrolls a contact. Emails send from the rep's personal Gmail or Outlook inbox. The sequence pauses instantly when the contact replies. Tasks remind reps to call, connect on LinkedIn, or take any custom action at the right moment.

From your SDR background

You know this system — you lived it every day. Sequences are your call cadence, formalised and automated. The 7-touch follow-up plan. The "email Monday, call Wednesday, LinkedIn Friday" process that the best SDRs ran consistently and others forgot halfway through. As admin, you're the person who builds that system once and gives it to the entire team.

Admin Setup · 05
Step-by-Step Setup
Path in HubSpot

Sales → Sequences → Create sequence

  1. Name the sequence — follow the convention: [Team] Purpose — Persona — Stage. Example: [SDR] Cold Outreach — SaaS VP — MQL. Clear naming lets reps pick the right sequence at enrolment without guessing.
  2. Add Step 1 — automated email — click "Add step" → Automated email → Create a new template or select an existing one. Write the subject line and email body. Add personalisation tokens. Keep the email to 3–5 sentences maximum.
  3. Set the delay after Step 1 — how many days until Step 2? For cold outreach: 2–3 days standard. Choose whether to send on business days only (strongly recommended for all outbound sequences).
  4. Add Step 2 — call task — click "+" → Phone call → Add task instructions: what should the rep say? What's the goal of this call? What to log in the call outcome field?
  5. Continue building steps — alternate between emails and task steps. Total steps: 5–8 for most sequences. More than 10 steps is usually too long — if there's no reply in 10 touches, you have your answer.
  6. Add the break-up email as the final step — every sequence should end with a "I'll close your file" email. This is your highest-converting email in cold outreach. Never skip it.
  7. Configure sequence settings — set "Send emails at a specific time" (business hours only), "Send on weekdays only," and whether to include an unsubscribe link.
  8. Review and save — save the sequence as a draft. Do a test enrolment on a colleague's contact to verify timing, template tokens, and task creation all work correctly before using with real prospects.

The break-up email template

Subject: Should I close your file, {{contact.firstname}}?

Hi {{contact.firstname}},

I've reached out a few times but haven't heard back — which usually means one of two things: the timing isn't right, or this isn't a priority right now.

Either way is completely fine. I'll close your file for now — but if anything changes, please don't hesitate to reach back out.

Wishing you all the best.

{{owner.firstname}}
Why the break-up email works

This email consistently generates the highest reply rate in any cold outreach sequence — often 2–4× higher than the opening email. People respond to finality. Either they engage (they were interested but kept forgetting) or they confirm they're not interested (which cleans your pipeline). Both outcomes are valuable. Never skip it.

The 7-Touch Cadence

The standard cold outreach cadence — 7 steps over 17 days. Adapt for your audience, but use this as the starting template.

  • E
    Day 0 — automated email
    Opening email — value proposition
    Short, personalised, specific to their situation. Opens with a relevant observation. Ends with a single meeting ask.
  • C
    Day 2 — call task
    First call attempt
    Reference the email. If voicemail: keep it under 20 seconds, mention the email, give your phone number twice.
  • E
    Day 4 — automated email
    Different angle — case study
    Different subject line, different opening. Lead with a relevant customer story. "We helped [Company like theirs] do X."
  • Li
    Day 7 — LinkedIn connect task
    LinkedIn connection request
    Short, personal note with the request. Reference something from their profile. No pitch in the connection request.
  • E
    Day 10 — automated email
    Social proof / competitor angle
    Different angle again — "If you're currently using [competitor], here's what our customers switched from and why."
  • C
    Day 14 — call task
    Second call attempt
    Last human touch before the break-up. Reference the case study email. Express genuine curiosity about their situation.
  • E
    Day 17 — automated email
    Break-up email
    "Should I close your file?" — finality drives replies. Generates 2–4× the reply rate of earlier emails. Never skip this step.

Enrollment Options

Two ways to enrol contacts — manual enrolment by reps, or automatic via workflows.

MethodHow it worksBest forAdmin setup
Manual enrolmentRep goes to Contact record → clicks "Enrol in sequence" → selects the sequence → reviews and optionally edits each automated email → confirms enrolmentWhen the rep needs to review and personalise each contact before enrolment. The standard method for outbound prospecting.Train reps on the enrolment review step — make sure they edit the opening email for each contact, not just bulk-enrol without personalisation.
Workflow enrolmentA workflow action "Enrol in sequence" automatically enrolls a contact when the workflow fires. Assigns the sequence to the contact's owner.When you want enrolment to happen automatically — e.g. MQL reaches a certain score and should immediately start an outreach sequenceSet the sequence owner carefully — the workflow assigns the sequence using the contact's owner property. Workflow enrolment risk

When enrolling contacts via workflow, they are automatically added without a rep reviewing or personalising the emails. This can result in generic, unedited emails going out at scale — which defeats the purpose of a personal sequence. Only use workflow enrolment if your templates are strong enough to send without per-contact editing, or if you're doing high-volume outreach where individual personalisation isn't feasible.

HubSpot shows reply rate per step — which email in the sequence generates the most replies. This is where most teams leave value on the table. Common patterns:

Case 1 — Sequences landing in spam
When HTML-heavy templates hurt deliverability
DeliverabilityB2B Tech

Problem
Solution

The problem

An SDR team's sequence open rates suddenly dropped from 42% to 11% over two weeks. Replies had effectively stopped. Investigation revealed two issues: the company's IT team had recently added a large HTML email signature template with a company banner image, tracking pixels, and multiple links — applied to every outgoing email including sequence emails. Additionally, several SDRs had added "click here to unsubscribe" footers styled as HTML with multiple hyperlinks. The email clients were routing these to the Promotions or Spam folder.

  • 1.
    Rebuilt all email templates as plain-text style — no HTML formatting, no banner images, no styled elements. Just: first name, body text, signature in plain text.
  • 2.
    Email signature standardised: Name, Title, Phone (plain text), LinkedIn URL (not hyperlinked, just the URL). No images, no HTML tables.
  • 3.
    Removed the HTML unsubscribe footer — for true 1-to-1 sequences under legal thresholds, unsubscribe links are optional. Kept them only where legally required.
  • 4.
    Reviewed email authentication: verified SPF, DKIM, and DMARC were properly configured for the company's domain. Found DMARC was in "none" mode — updated to "quarantine" after confirming all legitimate email sources were covered.
RevOps outcome

Open rates recovered from 11% to 38% within 2 weeks of template changes. Reply rates returned to baseline. Admin created a "Sequence Email Template Standards" document: no HTML elements, plain text signatures only, no embedded images. This document became part of every new SDR onboarding package. The IT team's HTML signature template was removed from sequence-sending accounts and kept only for non-sequence outbound email.

Applied Learning · 12
Common Admin Mistakes
  • 01 — One generic sequence for all personas
    A SaaS VP and a Retail Director have completely different priorities, vocabulary, and pain points. One generic sequence performs poorly for both. Build persona-specific sequences. Three targeted sequences will always outperform one generic one.
  • 02 — Sequences that are too long
    A 15-step sequence over 45 days is too long. Most replies come in the first 4 steps. After 6–7 touches with no response, you have your answer. Keep sequences under 8 steps. The break-up email should come within 3 weeks.
  • 03 — Email templates that are too long
    Sales emails are read in under 10 seconds. A 500-word product pitch will not be read. Every cold outreach email should be under 100 words. If you can't explain the value in 5 sentences, the message isn't clear enough — not the email isn't long enough.
  • 04 — Enrolling unqualified contacts
    Sequences are not a substitute for qualification. Enrolling every lead wastes rep time, hurts sender domain reputation, and inflates unsubscribe rates. Reserve sequences for contacts that have been reviewed and are genuinely worth pursuing.
  • 05 — HTML-heavy templates killing deliverability
    Images, HTML formatting, banner signatures, multiple hyperlinks — all increase spam classification risk. Sequence emails should look like they were written in 2 minutes in Gmail, not designed in Mailchimp. Plain text wins deliverability every time.
  • 06 — No break-up email as the final step
    The break-up email is typically the highest-converting email in any cold outreach sequence. Omitting it leaves your best opportunity on the table. Always end with a clear "I'll close your file" message.
  • 07 — Not reviewing step-level analytics
    Without step analytics, you don't know which email is working and which is failing. Reviewing reply rate by step monthly is what separates a sequence that was built once and forgotten from one that continuously improves.

Admin Best Practices
  • 📚
    Build a shared sequence library before reps start prospecting. Admin builds 3–5 core sequences for the main personas, publishes them as shared sequences. Reps select from the library — not build their own. One source of truth for outreach copy.
  • 📊
    Review step analytics monthly. Which step gets the most replies? That's the signal. Move what's working earlier. Rewrite or remove what isn't generating engagement.
  • 📧
    Keep all templates plain text. No HTML, no banner images, no formatted signatures. Sequence emails should look like a human wrote them in their email client, not designed in a marketing tool.
  • ✉️
    Always include a break-up email as the final step. Every cold outreach sequence, no exceptions. The finality generates replies that nothing else does.
  • 📏
    Test subject lines systematically. Run the same sequence with two different subject lines for 30 days. Pick the winner by open rate. Standardise. Repeat.
  • 🎯
    Use sequence analytics for rep coaching. Reply rate by rep in the same sequence reveals skill gaps — not lead quality differences. Low reply rate from specific reps points to personalisation or targeting issues worth coaching.
  • ⏸️
    Pause sequences before major holidays and company events. Sequences continue running unless you stop them. Build a quarterly calendar of pause dates and make sure someone is responsible for pausing active sequences before major breaks.

  4. Confirm enrolment. The first email should send (or attempt to send from your connected inbox) immediately.
  5. Go to Sales → Sequences → find your sequence. Check the Enrolled count and see the contact listed.
  6. On the Contact record: check the timeline. You should see the sequence enrolment and the first email logged as an activity.

Interview Questions
"What is the difference between a HubSpot Sequence and a Workflow?"
They solve different problems at different scales. A Sequence is a personalised 1-to-1 sales outreach tool — emails send from the rep's personal Gmail or Outlook inbox, they feel like real human emails, and the entire sequence stops automatically the moment the contact replies. They're for SDR cold outreach, AE follow-up cadences, and CSM personal touchpoints with specific accounts. Workflows are a bulk automation engine — emails send from HubSpot's marketing system to hundreds or thousands of contacts at once, they don't stop on reply, and they can combine emails with property updates, task creation, and CRM record changes in one flow. The best RevOps setup uses both: workflows handle the systemic automation and lifecycle management, sequences handle the personal outreach when a rep is actively working a prospect. They're complementary tools — workflows bring contacts to sequences' door, sequences take them through a personal conversation.
"How would you build a sequence library for a new SDR team?"
First I'd work with sales leadership to map the key personas SDRs will be targeting and what stage they're at — cold MQL outreach, post-demo follow-up, re-engagement of lost deals. For each persona, I'd define the core pain point angle, a relevant social proof example, and the tone that matches the audience. I'd draft the 7-step template structure and get feedback from the best-performing rep before building in HubSpot. Then I'd build 3–5 sequences as shared sequences with clear naming: [SDR] Cold — SaaS VP — MQL, [SDR] Cold — FinTech Director — MQL. I'd connect the MQL review workflow to include a task that prompts the SDR to review the contact's persona and enrol in the right sequence. After 30 days I'd pull step-level analytics — which email generates the most replies? That email gets moved earlier in the sequence. Underperforming emails get rewritten or removed. The library evolves based on data, not intuition.
"A rep's sequence emails are landing in spam. How do you investigate and fix it?"
I'd start with the email templates themselves — the most common cause is HTML-heavy templates with images, formatted signatures, or multiple hyperlinks. Spam filters treat these like marketing emails. I'd rebuild the templates in plain text style: no formatting, text-only signature. Then I'd check the sending domain's email authentication: SPF, DKIM, and DMARC records. Misconfigured authentication means receiving servers can't verify the email is legitimate. I'd also check the bounce rate for recent sequence sends — high bounce rates indicate either low-quality contact lists or domain reputation problems. If bounce rate is high, I'd clean the contact list and reduce sending volume temporarily. Finally I'd check whether the rep's Gmail or Outlook account has had any spam complaints flagged — too many complaints over a short period damage sender reputation and need time to recover through reduced volume and high-quality sends.
"What makes the break-up email so effective, and where do you place it?"
The break-up email works because it leverages finality — people respond to closure in a way they don't respond to a sixth follow-up. A contact who has been passively interested but too busy to reply suddenly finds urgency when they feel the opportunity is about to close. It also filters the pipeline: contacts who genuinely aren't interested confirm it, which is also valuable — it cleans your active pipeline rather than letting dead leads linger. I place it as the final step in every cold outreach sequence — typically after 5–6 earlier touches, within 3 weeks of the first email. The data consistently shows break-up emails generate the highest reply rate of any step in the sequence, often 3–5× higher than the opening email. I've also found that moving a softer version of the break-up tone into an earlier step — around step 3 — increases overall sequence reply rate significantly, because that direct, low-pressure tone is what the audience responds to throughout, not just at the end.

RevOps mindset on sequences

The sequence library is intellectual property — the accumulated outreach intelligence of your sales organisation. The templates that generate replies, the step order that converts, the subject lines that get opened — all represent months of iteration and thousands of data points. An admin who builds, tests, and systematically optimises the sequence library is contributing directly to pipeline generation. Sequence analytics also connect directly to rep coaching: reply rate by rep in the same sequence reveals execution and personalisation skill gaps, not lead quality problems. This is where sales enablement and RevOps converge — the admin who owns the sequence library is influencing how every deal starts.
